What is a wombat?

A short-legged, muscular quadrupedal marsupial that is native to Australia.

ㅤㅤCommon Name: Wombat

ㅤㅤScientific Name: Vombatus ursinus

ㅤㅤType: Mammals

ㅤㅤKingdom: Animalia

ㅤㅤDiet: Herbivore

ㅤㅤGroup Name: Mob, colony, wisdom

ㅤㅤSize: 28 to 47 inches

ㅤㅤWeight: 32 to 80 pounds

Types of Wombats

Bare-Nosed Wombat

Their fur can be of various shades of grey and black. It sometimes presents with grey or beige-coloured patches. Unlike the other types of wombats, it does not have hair on the rhinarium (the area around the nostrils). It is also distinguished by its smaller, hairier ears. They tend to be smaller than the other species, but there are exceptions. Individuals of this type inhabit Tasmania and other areas.

Southern Hairy-Nosed Wombat

The distinctive feature of the southern wombat is that its nasal bone is longer than the frontal bone. Their hair is silky in texture and the colour ranges from grey to tan. The rhinarium is covered in smooth white fur and the snout is longer and more square than that of the common wombat.

Northern Hairy-Nosed Wombat

The fur of this species is silky and brown. It tends to be heavier than the previous species. Its rhinarium is also covered in hair and has a wider muzzle than other types of wombat.

Life Cycle

  • First born

    When first born, wombats weigh only one gram and their mouths are attached to the mother's teat. At this stage they are furless and wildlife carers refer to them as "Pinkies”.

  • Stay in the pouch

    Wombats are marsupials and carry their young (joeys) in the mother's pouch until they are big enough to be independent.

  • Leave the pouch

    Young wombats leave the pouch at about five months old, and care for themselves (are independent) at seven months.

Habitat and self-defense

  • Habitat

    They use big claws to dig large burrows. They Spend up to 4 days sleeping in one burrows before moving onto another, creating intricate tunnel-and-chamber complexes which are often used by other small mammals for shleter as well.

  • Self-defense

    They are well-equipped to protect themselves from predators. In their hind quarters, their sturdy backbone plates are surrounded by tough cartilage, fat, and fur-forming a durable shield.

  • Threat

    When under threat, wombats dive into their burrows and use their rear ends to block the entrance and protect the rest of their bodies. They may even land a hefty kick on their attacker for good measure.

Wombats in danger

  • 01Mange infection

    Mange breakout affecting the southern hairy-nosed wombat, or even the critically endangered northern hairy-nosed wombat, could cause extinction. Infected wombats slowly succumb to the complications of the disease within 2-3 months, leading to a slow and painful death.

  • 02Vehicle collisions

    Each year in Australia many thousands of collisions occur between motor vehicles and animals, resulting in considerable vehicle repair costs, injury to persons, and loss of animal life. Wombats are now ranked third by motorists as the most accidentally struck animal.

  • 03Habitat loss

    There are a limited number of places to establish new populations of wombats in the future.

  • 04Predation

    Previous records have shown predation by wild dogs is a considerable threat to the northern hairy-nosed wombat.

  • 05Floods

    Floods are a potential threat to populations of northern hairy-nosed wombats, due to their ability to inundate burrow systems and destroy food resources.

  • 06Drought

    Drought, such as the six-year-long drought in the 1990s, slows the reproductive output of northern hairy-nosed wombats and reduces body condition and survival rates in the population. A study by Crossman et al. (1994) found 50-75% of breeding females successfully bred when good summer rainfall occurred, whilst only 20% bred during a major drought.

  • 07Wildfire

    Wildfire is not a direct threat to individual northern hairy-nosed wombats as deep burrows provide protection. However, fire will destroy food resources and may impact particular tree species whose roots provide stability in wombat burrows.

Fun facts about wombats

  • Do they poop in cubes?

    One of the more unusual facts about wombats is that their poo resembles an 80s toy craze rather than actual animal faeces. No joke: these critters excrete cube-shaped poop! This stops the poo from rolling off their marked territory of rocks or trees. Glad we got to the bottom of that…

  • Is their butt the main form of defence?

    The wombats' rear end is their main form of defence against predators. When a predator is near, these big booty mammals dive down their burrows and block off the entrance with their butts. As their booties mainly consist of cartilage, they are very resistant to scratches and bites. They can also crush predators' skulls with their behinds. Crazy stuff.

  • Are koalas the closest relative of wombats?

    Although Koalas could be physically compared to bears, pigs and guinea pigs, the closest living relative to the Wombat is the Koala. Although they may not look physically similar, both species have backwards-facing pouches. This is especially useful for Wombats as they won't get dirt in their pouches while digging.

  • Is a group of wombats known as wisdom?

    A wisdom of wombats. A mob of kangaroos. A paddle of platypus. We can't get enough of Australian animal collective nouns!

  • How much does the heaviest wombat weigh?

    The Heaviest Wombat Was 38kg. The oldest (31 years) and heaviest wombat was Patrick the Bare-nosed Wombat who lived at Ballarat Wildlife Park. This makes him the weight of around four Koalas combined.

  • How fast can wombats run?

    These furry marsupials sure are stocky, but don't let that fool you, they can run at speeds up to 40 kilometres per hour which is just under retired sprinter Usain Bolt's fastest recorded speed.

  • How long do wombat's teeth grow?

    Their teeth never stop growing, which ensures they don't grind away when they are munching on grasses, roots, shrubs and vegetables.

  • How long can wombat's guts hold food?

    Their Guts Can Hold Food For 70 Hours and it takes them around four to six days to digest a meal.

  • Is is true that the wombats are the second largest of all marsupials?

    Yes, it is. The largest living marsupial is the Red Kangaroo. However, the largest ever marsupial was a Diprotodon, which was a wombat-like creature standing at around 2 metres tall.
